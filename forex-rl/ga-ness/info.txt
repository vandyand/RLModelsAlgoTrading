## What You're Actually Going to Build

Imagine you're trying to find the perfect recipe for chocolate chip cookies. You have hundreds of variables - how much flour, sugar, butter, baking time, temperature, etc. Instead of trying every single combination (which would take forever), you're going to use "evolution" to find the best recipe.

Here's how this works in trading: You want to find the perfect combination of trading rules that make money on EUR_USD. Instead of testing every possible combination of indicators and parameters (which is literally impossible), you're going to let the computer evolve better and better trading strategies.

## Step 1: What is a "Trading Strategy" in Computer Terms?

This is where most people get confused, so let me be super concrete. A trading strategy, to a computer, is just a bunch of numbers and rules. Let's start with the simplest possible example:

**Simple Moving Average Strategy:**
- If 10-day average > 20-day average, BUY
- If 10-day average < 20-day average, SELL

To the computer, this strategy is just three numbers:
1. First moving average period: 10
2. Second moving average period: 20  
3. Rule: 1 (meaning "buy when first > second")

## Step 2: How Do You Turn Numbers Into Actual Trades?

This is the "fitness function" - it's literally a piece of code that takes your strategy numbers and simulates trading with them. Here's what it does:

1. **Takes your strategy**: ``[2][1]
2. **Calculates the indicators**: Computes 10-day and 20-day moving averages for every day
3. **Applies the rules**: When 10-day > 20-day, record a "BUY" signal
4. **Simulates trading**: Pretend you bought/sold based on those signals
5. **Calculates profit**: How much money would you have made?
6. **Returns a score**: Higher score = better strategy

The genetic algorithm doesn't understand trading at all. It just knows "strategy A got a score of 15%, strategy B got 8%, so A is better than B."

## Step 4: Your Specific Problem - Why It's Hard

Now, your problem is way more complex than just two moving averages. You have:

**68 different currency pairs** - Each could be an input to your strategy
**Hundreds of possible indicators** - RSI, MACD, Bollinger Bands, etc.
**Different time periods** - 5-minute bars, 15-minute bars, etc.
**Complex rules** - "Buy if EUR_USD RSI > 70 AND GBP_USD MACD is rising AND..."

So instead of a strategy being ``, it might be something like:[2][1]
`[EUR_USD, RSI, 14, >, 70, AND, GBP_USD, MACD, 12, 26, rising, BUY]`

This creates two big problems:

**Problem 1: The search space is enormous.** With 68 pairs and dozens of indicators, you could have millions or billions of possible combinations. Your computer would run for years trying to test them all.

**Problem 2: How do you represent complex rules as numbers?** The moving average example was easy because it's just three numbers. But "If EUR_USD RSI > 70 AND GBP_USD MACD is rising" is much more complex.

## Step 5: Practical Solutions (What You Actually Need to Do)

**Solution 1: Start Simple and Build Up**

Don't try to solve the whole problem at once. Start with something manageable:

**Week 1:** Just EUR_USD with 3 indicators (RSI, EMA, MACD)
- Your strategy could be: `[RSI_period, RSI_buy_threshold, RSI_sell_threshold, EMA_period, use_MACD_yes_or_no]`
- This gives you maybe 1000 possible combinations instead of billions

**Week 2:** Add one more currency pair (maybe GBP_USD)
- Now you can have rules like "Buy EUR_USD when GBP_USD RSI is also high"

**Week 3:** Add more complexity gradually

**Why this works:** You learn how to implement the basic genetic algorithm on a simple problem first. Once that's working, you can scale it up.

**Solution 2: Use "Feature Selection" Instead of "Rule Generation"**

Instead of trying to evolve complex rules, just evolve which features to pay attention to. 

Here's how this works:
1. **Pre-calculate everything:** Calculate RSI, MACD, EMA, etc. for all 68 currency pairs
2. **Create a giant table:** Each row is a 5-minute time period, each column is a different indicator/pair combination
3. **Use genetic algorithm to pick columns:** The GA just decides which columns are useful and which to ignore
4. **Use simple rules:** Once you know which indicators matter, use basic thresholds like "buy when selected indicators average > 0.7"

**Pros:** Much simpler to implement, faster to run, easier to understand results
**Cons:** Limits you to simpler trading logic

**Solution 3: Tree-Based Representation (Advanced)**

This is where you represent strategies as "decision trees" that the computer can modify. But honestly, I'd skip this until you get the simpler approaches working.

## The Biggest Gotcha: Overfitting

Here's the thing that will bite you: genetic algorithms are really good at finding patterns in historical data that don't actually work in the future. It's like finding a recipe that works perfectly for last Tuesday's weather but fails every other day.

**How to avoid this:**
- Always keep some data hidden from the GA for final testing
- Don't let strategies get too complex (limit the number of parameters)
- Test strategies on different time periods
- If something looks too good to be true, it probably is

## Why This Approach Might Work Better Than Deep Learning

**Pros:**
- Much faster to run than training neural networks
- Results are interpretable (you can see exactly what rules it found)
- Doesn't need huge amounts of data
- Less prone to catastrophic failure
- Easier to debug when something goes wrong

**Cons:**
- Might miss complex patterns that neural networks could find
- Still vulnerable to overfitting
- Requires more manual feature engineering
- Can get stuck in local optima
